# 🇦🇱 Albania‘s Digital Economy & Investment Landscape (2026)

Albania has emerged as one of Southeast Europe's most **dynamic regulatory stories**—a nation of approximately 2.75 million people that is rapidly transitioning from "digital catch-up" to "digital architecture" . For an investor with "millions," Albania offers a compelling proposition: a **15% flat corporate income tax** (with effective 0% in technology parks for up to 15 years), **open banking now operational**, **SEPA accession achieved** (November 2024), **instant payments being built** (TIPS clone from Bank of Italy), and an **e‑commerce market projected to grow from $0.72 billion (2026) to $1.33 billion by 2031** at a **12.99% CAGR** .

Unlike the fully EU-regulated markets of Croatia and Slovenia, Albania is an **EU candidate country** moving at its own pace—but moving decisively. The Bank of Albania has positioned itself as a "regional pioneer in open banking" after embracing PSD2-style reforms . In November 2024, the first open banking license was granted to a financial entity, marking a practical turning point . Albania joined the **Single Euro Payments Area (SEPA)** schemes in November 2024, with electronic payments increasing by **24% year-on-year** in the first half of 2025 .

For Japanese investors specifically, the critical headline is that **Albania has a Double Taxation Agreement with Japan** that HMRC regards as containing an "appropriate non-discrimination article" . While specific reduced rates for dividends, interest, and royalties require consultation with the treaty text, this positions Albania alongside Serbia, Croatia, and Slovenia as DTA-covered jurisdictions—unlike Kosovo and Montenegro (though Montenegro has negotiations underway).

Albania joined the **geographical scope of SEPA schemes in November 2024** . This integration with European payment infrastructure is a game-changer for cross-border e-commerce and fintech operations, allowing euro-denominated payments to flow as efficiently as within EU member states.

### Technology Parks – The "Durana" Controversy and Opportunity

In April 2026, the Albanian parliament passed amendments to the **Law on Technology Parks** (Law 58/2022), known as the "Durana Law," which created significant controversy .

**Fiscal incentives offered to technology park companies** :

| Incentive | Duration |
|-----------|----------|
| **0% profit tax** | 15 years |
| **0% payroll tax** | 10 years |
| **0% VAT** (goods, equipment, services) | Indefinite |
| **Customs duties exemption** | Indefinite |
| **Social security benefits** | Indefinite |
| **Local tax exemption** | 10 years |
| **Partial staff training reimbursement** | State-funded |
| **Simplified customs procedures** | Indefinite |

**The controversy**: Opposition MPs have denounced the law as creating a "total tax haven" that:
- Allows **virtual registration** of companies without physical presence
- Contradicts OECD-BEPS standards requiring profits to be taxed where real economic value is created 
- Violates EU principles of fair competition 
- Creates ideal conditions for **shell companies** contrary to EU Anti-Shell directives 

**The opportunity for investors**: Despite political controversy, these incentives are **legally enacted** and available to qualifying technology companies. The 0% profit tax for 15 years is among the most generous in Europe—but investors should conduct thorough due diligence on eligibility requirements and monitor potential EU challenges.
